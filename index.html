Our Research

From Manual Assessment to AI-Driven Decisions

Sri Lanka’s tea sector is a major agricultural export industry and a key source of foreign-exchange earnings, yet operational decision-making remains constrained by manual assessment and incomplete digitisation.

Project Scope

Develop a multi-modal AI framework addressing four prediction tasks aligned with practical actions from field to auction: freshness grading from mobile imagery, yield digitisation and forecasting from estate records, auction-oriented demand forecasting from historical Colombo tea auction data, and field health-risk recognition from images linked to climate data.

Literature Survey

AI in agriculture has moved from isolated prediction models to integrated decision-support systems. In tea production, AI has been applied to freshness grading, yield forecasting, auction price prediction, and plant health monitoring. However, most studies rely on laboratory-style datasets, limiting deployment reliability in real plantation and market conditions.

Literature Survey Visual Representation

Manual tea leaf grading is subjective and inconsistent. Early automation showed fresh-leaf sorting was feasible in controlled settings, and CNNs improved quality assessment, but robustness drops under field variation. Statistical models like ARIMA remain common for yield forecasting, while hybrid approaches combining simulation with ML improve performance. OCR is widely studied but end-to-end integration into agricultural analytics is limited. Tea auctions are difficult to predict due to volatility, and most work treats visual disease detection and climate-linked outbreak prediction separately.

Research Gap

Laboratory-centric grading datasets

Most grading studies rely on laboratory-style datasets, limiting deployment reliability in plantations. This motivates standardised mobile image capture and stronger preprocessing to reduce illumination shifts and background noise.

Incomplete digitisation of estate records

Many estates depend on handwritten records. OCR has been widely studied for automated text extraction, but end-to-end integration into agricultural analytics pipelines is still limited, hindering yield forecasting accuracy.

Auction demand inferred, not measured

Many demand models assume direct demand observations, while in tea auctions demand is often inferred from bidding dynamics rather than measured explicitly. Non-stationarity and temporal structure further complicate prediction.

Separate disease and climate models

Most work treats visual disease detection and climate-linked outbreak prediction separately. For elevation-stratified Sri Lankan plantations, integrated frameworks combining visual health detection, invasive species monitoring, and seasonal risk modelling remain limited.

Research Problem

How to automate tea quality and production decisions from field to auction?

Deployment-faithful modelling is shaped by three recurring reliability constraints: (1) Domain shift across estates, devices, and illumination in mobile imagery, and across scan quality and layout variation in historical records; (2) Temporal non-stationarity in production and market targets, requiring time-ordered evaluation; (3) Class imbalance where minority classes carry disproportionate operational risk, requiring imbalance-aware reporting because aggregate accuracy can obscure failures on rare but consequential outcomes.

Main Objective

Integrated AI Decision-Support for Sri Lanka’s Tea Sector

Develop a multi-modal AI framework that automates key decision-making processes in Sri Lanka’s tea industry by leveraging deep learning for four tasks: freshness grading and tea-type classification from mobile-captured leaf images, yield forecasting using estate records and scanned logbooks, auction demand prediction based on historical transaction data, and disease/pest detection from images with seasonal risk prediction linked to climate data. The system must remain robust to heterogeneous capture conditions and record formats, enabling early detection, accurate forecasting, and informed decision-making as a scalable solution to improve productivity, quality, and market competitiveness.

Specific Objectives

01

Freshness grading and tea-type classification from mobile-captured leaf images using YOLOv8 + SAHI detection and explainable ML grading.

02

Yield digitisation and forecasting using scanned estate logbooks with OCR, fuzzy matching, and structured data extraction.

03

Auction-oriented demand forecasting using historical Colombo tea auction transactions with multi-agent RL simulation and ARIMA models.

04

Field health-risk recognition from images under label scarcity and class imbalance, with seasonal risk prediction linked to climate data.

Four Researchers, Four Components

AI-Driven Tea Quality and Production Improvements — built from four complementary research components, each owned and engineered by a team member.

IT22154576

Tea Leaf Freshness Grading & Inspection

Nipuna Abeysekara

YOLOv8s + SAHI multi-class detection (7 classes), explainable binary grading via 25 OpenCV features feeding 10 classical ML models, plus a RAG-powered Ceylon Tea knowledge base over 156 documents and 473 chunks. 275 leaf images collected across 66 field sessions in mid-country estates.

Harsha Waduthanthri

Vision-based OCR pipeline that converts physical tea-yield logbook pages into structured Excel spreadsheets. Cell-grid detection, per-cell crop recognition, 5-algorithm fuzzy matching ensemble, and template-driven export to digitise estate records for yield forecasting.
